Project overview

The problem with
fragmented finance

People managing their money in 2025 use an average of three to four separate apps — a banking app, a P2P payment tool, a budgeting spreadsheet, and something for tracking goals. Every app switch adds friction, and every time they can't find a transaction or a budget number, anxiety spikes.

Paylo was designed to eliminate that fragmentation: one app for payments, transfers, and budgeting — fast enough for the person who just wants to send money to a friend, and organized enough for the person who needs to see where every dollar went this month.

01 — Research

Understanding the real pain

I conducted a mixed-method research phase with 12 survey participants and 5 in-depth interviews with adults aged 25–45 who regularly use payment and budgeting apps. The goal was not to validate assumptions — it was to uncover what genuinely causes stress, confusion, and distrust in financial tools.

One finding surfaced in every session: managing finances feels harder than it should. Users weren't struggling because of a lack of features — they were overwhelmed by the number of places they had to look.

🔍
Transparency
Users want real-time progress, visible fees ($0.00 displayed before confirmation), and clear transaction statuses. Vague "Processing…" messages triggered anxiety that money was lost.
⚡
Speed builds trust
Slow transfers were tied directly to distrust. David (data analyst) checked his accounts multiple times per day to confirm transactions had landed — a behavior driven entirely by app lag.
🗂️
Simplicity
25% of survey respondents cited confusing navigation as their top frustration. Technical jargon and too many steps made users feel incompetent, not uninformed.
🎯
One app expectation
75% of participants expressed a strong preference for replacing multiple tools with a single unified app. Olivia (event planner) switched between 3 apps daily just to track her budget.

Two types of user,
one unified solution

Research revealed a clear split: users who primarily move money (Transactors) and users who primarily analyze money (Budgeters). Paylo had to serve both without compromising on either.

03 — Journey mapping

Where the emotional pain lives

Mapping Brandon's P2P payment journey revealed that frustration peaked not during the transfer itself, but in the waiting period afterward — the absence of clear feedback created more anxiety than the act of sending money. This directly informed the 3-step payment flow with instant confirmation.

04 — Design process

From assumption to evidence

1
Information architecture + card sorting
The first sitemap was built on assumptions and landed at just 32% accuracy. I ran a closed card sorting test with 8 participants using Optimal Workshop to understand how users actually group financial tasks. The result was a restructured sitemap with a hard divide between Payments (action) and Budget (analysis) — the split that users naturally expected but no existing app had implemented clearly.
2
Low-fidelity wireframes
Lo-fi focused purely on layout and information hierarchy. Each screen was centered on a single primary task to minimize cognitive load. Sketched both mobile (4-tab bottom nav) and desktop (side-by-side panel) layouts, validating the dual-persona model — fast vertical flows for Brandon, parallel deep-dive panels for Elaine.
3
Mid-fidelity prototype + usability testing
Mid-fi introduced spacing, visual grouping, and the 3-step P2P payment flow. Usability testing with 5 participants across two days (Jan–Feb 2026) focused on task completion, error rates, and emotional response during high-risk moments like payment confirmation. The $0.00 fee label and instant transaction ID were added directly in response to testing observations.
4
High-fidelity prototype
Hi-fi introduced brand visuals, realistic data, and consistent interaction patterns. Animated transitions and clear visual hierarchy guide users through end-to-end flows. The key learning: high fidelity is about refining clarity and trust, not adding decoration. Small details — wording, feedback timing, visual hierarchy — proved to have an outsized effect on user confidence in a finance context.

06 — Prototype evolution

Three fidelities,
one clear direction

Establishing structure and hierarchy
Lo-fi focused on layout decisions, not visual design. Each screen was constrained to a single primary task. The 4-tab bottom navigation (Home, Payments, Budget, Account) was validated at this stage — grounding the app in the card sort findings.
4-tab bottom navigation validated against card sort results
Mobile-first: single-task screens to reduce cognitive load
Desktop: full-width layouts with parallel panels for Elaine's use case
Content grouping tested with paper prototypes before digitizing
Improving usability through clarity
Mid-fi introduced spacing, alignment, and visual grouping. The 3-step payment flow was implemented here and tested in usability sessions. Status pills (Active, Pending) and the $0.00 fee label were added in direct response to testing observations — users needed to see transparency, not just trust that it was there.
3-step P2P flow: Start → Amount → Confirm (validated in testing)
$0.00 fee prominently displayed before final confirmation tap
Onboarding coach marks to address the 25% navigation frustration
Standardized status pills replacing vague "Processing" states
Visual progress bars for budget goals replacing static number tables
Building trust through visual polish
Hi-fi introduced brand visuals, realistic data, and consistent interaction patterns. The key insight from this phase: high fidelity means refining clarity, not adding complexity. Checkbox toggles became switch controls for security settings. Color-coded budget status (safe/warning/exceeded) replaced manual calculation. Every screen was optimized so the most important action was immediately visible without scrolling.
Animated step indicators guide users through the payment flow
Color-coded budget status system: safe (green), warning (amber), exceeded (red)
Triple-locked security: Biometrics + PIN + Alerts visible in Account tab
Responsive design: mobile vertical flow ↔ desktop parallel panel layout
Dedicated support panel during high-risk actions (payments) to reduce anxiety

08 — Reflections

What I learned

On prototyping
High fidelity doesn't mean adding more decoration or interactions — it means refining clarity and trust. Moving from mid-fi to hi-fi showed me how much small details like wording, feedback timing, and visual hierarchy affect user confidence, especially in finance-related products.
On research
The card sort was humbling. The original sitemap felt logical to me — but 32% accuracy proved that what makes sense to a designer rarely matches how real users organize information. The data taught me to distrust my assumptions early and test them cheaply.
On scope
The biggest temptation in hi-fi was scope creep — adding new features instead of polishing core flows. Resisting that and focusing on the Send Money flow, the Budget overview, and the security hub produced a much more coherent, trustworthy product.
Next steps
I would validate the design through usability testing focused on key tasks — sending money and reviewing budgets — measuring task completion time, error rates, and user confidence during high-risk moments like confirmations. I'd also test whether transaction status and spending insights are understood without additional explanation.
